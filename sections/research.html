Stars

Stellar interiors · Differential rotation · Meridional circulation

Overview

Standard stellar evolution codes, such as MESA, treat stars as rigidly-rotating one-dimensional objects, a simplification that discards the rich three-dimensional fluid dynamics of their interiors. In reality, stars exhibit differential rotation, where different latitudes and depths rotate at different rates, alongside large-scale meridional circulation, a slow overturning flow driven by imbalances between rotation and thermal stratification. These flows mix chemical species across layers that would otherwise remain stratified, alter angular momentum transport, and shape the long-term evolutionary trajectory of the star.

Rotation Profiles and Angular Momentum Transport

My first paper in this line of work produced analytical solutions to time-dependent internal rotation profiles in Sun-like stars. We used the principle of "downward control" from atmospheric physics to recover standard stellar physics results, where radiative diffusion drives the penetration of differential rotation into stratified interiors. These pen-and-paper solutions make it possible to probe the feasibility of enhanced angular momentum transport in stellar evolution calculations, offering a tractable benchmark for how transport prescriptions in codes like MESA perform against the full fluid-dynamical picture.

Compositional Gradients and Solar Abundances

The second paper extended this framework to include the role of molecular weight gradients, an effect that arises wherever chemical composition varies with depth. I showed that these gradients feed back on the structure of meridional circulation in ways that carry measurable consequences: they influence elemental abundances just below the solar convective zone, and modify the long-term temporal evolution of the circulation itself. This connects internal fluid dynamics to spectroscopic observables and helioseismic inferences.

Methods

Both papers combine analytic reduction of the governing equations with numerical integration on the pseudospectral solver Dedalus. The reduced-order formulations keep the physics tractable enough to survey parameter space and draw physical intuition, while the full numerical solutions validate the analytic results and capture effects that resist closed-form treatment.

Planets

Exoplanetary atmospheres · Variable irradiation · Atmospheric dynamics

Overview

This line of work constitutes the first generalized theoretical investigation of planets that receive variable radiation due to a range of configurational effects. Both tidally locked and asynchronously rotating planets are considered, covering the full diversity of short-period exoplanets. The unifying theme is that stellar forcing on these worlds is not constant, and understanding how their atmospheres respond to that variability unlocks new observable signatures and climate physics.

Time-Dependent Climate Response

Using shallow water models of planetary atmospheres solved on the pseudospectral code Dedalus, I derived analytical expressions for day-night temperature contrasts as time-dependent responses to variable stellar forcing. This framework reveals novel climate states and modes of variability that are absent from steady-state models. In particular, asynchronously rotating planets exhibit synchronous circulation patterns driven by a resonance between the instellation and diurnal periods, leading to permanent hot and cold sides separated by what I call "therminators". This phenomenon has implications for the habitability of these worlds, potentially extending the boundaries of the habitable zone of their star.

Observational Signatures

The most consequential finding from this work is the impact of irradiation variability on astronomical observables. Phase curves, which encode the spatially resolved thermal emission of a planet as it orbits its star, carry imprints of the circulation state and its temporal evolution. The signatures identified in this work may be detectable with current observatories and future missions, providing a path toward testing these theoretical predictions observationally.

Magnetic Fields of Ultra-Hot Jupiters

In a parallel observational thread, I was co-PI on an approved IGRINS observing program targeting ultra-hot Jupiter atmospheres. The goal is to constrain magnetic field strengths using 3D general circulation models combined with high-resolution cross-correlation spectroscopy. Magnetic fields can suppress atmospheric circulation and alter chemical mixing, and detecting them is a key step toward a complete physical picture of these extreme worlds.

Asteroids

Granular flows · Rubble-pile dynamics · Spin evolution

Overview

Many small solar system bodies, including the well-known asteroids Bennu and Ryugu visited by OSIRIS-REx and Hayabusa2, are rubble piles: loose aggregates of rock and regolith held together by self-gravity and surface friction rather than material strength. Their characteristic top-like shapes are thought to emerge from surface mass redistribution driven by YORP spin-up, a process that triggers granular flows and eventual mass shedding from the equatorial region.

Granular Flows and Spin Evolution

My M.Tech thesis developed models for the continuum mechanics of regolith flowing on the surface of a spinning, gravitating body. Using finite volume codes I wrote for non-Newtonian granular rheology, I traced how material migrates equatorward under centrifugal forcing, how it reshapes the body's surface, and how the resulting mass shedding feeds back on the spin state of the asteroid. This two-way coupling between surface flow and rotation is what drives the long-term spin evolution of top-shaped bodies. Companion work extended the modeling to idealized ellipsoidal geometries to isolate the role of body shape in controlling flow dynamics.

Methods

The computational work employed both continuum finite volume approaches treating regolith as a non-Newtonian fluid, and discrete element methods tracking individual grains, enabling cross-validation between the two frameworks. The continuum codes were developed from scratch and run on high-performance computing systems. This work trained a suite of skills in grid-based numerical methods, computational rheology, and small-body geophysics that have since informed my broader approach to computational fluid dynamics.

Agentic Coding, LLMs & Others

Symbolic Regression and Physical Law Discovery

As a computational astrophysicist, I spend most of my time writing numerical simulations to study fluid dynamics in stars and planets. Recently, I explored a question at the boundary of scientific computing and machine learning: can an ML tool rediscover a known physical law purely from simulation data, without being told what to look for? This project was completed entirely within Claude Code, from problem formulation to running simulations, generating the dataset, and finally applying symbolic regression to extract the underlying equation.

I used Dedalus, the pseudospectral PDE solver I use in my research, to simulate inertia-gravity waves in the rotating shallow water system, a foundational model for planetary atmospheres and oceans. I ran 72 simulations across a grid of physical parameters (rotation rate, gravity, fluid depth, and wavenumber), measuring the dominant wave frequency from each run using a Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) module. The data covered regimes from non-rotating pure gravity waves to strongly rotation-dominated inertial oscillations, both of which naturally originate from the governing equations.

I then fed the resulting dataset, 72 input-output pairs with no equation labels, into PySR, a symbolic regression library that searches for compact mathematical expressions. Starting from scratch, PySR rediscovered the exact inertia-gravity wave dispersion relation, ω = √(f² + gHk²), at complexity 9 with near-machine-precision accuracy. The five-order-of-magnitude drop in error at that step is a striking demonstration that symbolic regression can serve as a hypothesis-generation tool for physical systems where the governing equations are not fully known. LLM Evaluation in Physics

As part of my interest in agentic AI and its role in scientific discovery, I have contributed to designing evaluation benchmarks for large language models in physics. One thread involved constructing counterfactual physics problems as probes of scientific reasoning: questions designed to test whether a model can apply physical principles in unfamiliar or hypothetical configurations, rather than pattern-match against memorized solutions. I was also a contributor to Humanity's Last Exam, a large-scale expert-level scientific benchmark published in Nature in 2025, which assesses frontier AI models across disciplines including physics.

Currently, I am working on using coding agents like Claude Code to self-consistently run simulations of various astrophysical scenarios and implement symbolic regression techniques to extract interpretable physical laws from the resulting data. This line of work is still in its early stages, but it represents an exciting frontier at the intersection of AI and physics, with the potential to accelerate discovery and deepen our understanding of complex systems.

Mechanical Logic Gates

My B.Tech thesis, carried out in collaboration with the Computational Design Laboratory at IISc Bangalore, focused on the theoretical design and macroscale prototyping of a mechanical OR gate using pinned-pinned bistable arches. These compliant mechanisms exploit the elastic snap-through instability of pre-buckled beams to perform binary logic without electronics or actuation. The macroscale prototype served as a proof of concept for eventual miniaturization to MEMS-scale devices, where passive mechanical computation could find application in extreme or low-power environments. This work sits at the intersection of structural mechanics, nonlinear dynamics, and compliant mechanism design.
